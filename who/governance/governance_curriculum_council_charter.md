# Curriculum Council Charter

## Mandate

The Curriculum Council is the standing body responsible for the design, maintenance, evaluation, and evolution of the Context Commons curriculum. It ensures that curriculum content is rigorous, community-governed, culturally responsive, and technically sound.

The Council does not operate the program. It governs what is taught, how competency is measured, and what ethical guardrails apply to community-built agent systems. Program operations are managed by pilot site stewards and the campaign execution hierarchy.

## Authority

The Council has authority over:

1. **Curriculum framework** — track structures (Core, Builder, Steward, Elder), module sequences, prerequisite chains
2. **Competency milestones** — what participants must demonstrate at each stage to progress
3. **Ethical guardrails** — what community-built agents should and should not do; safety boundaries for youth-built systems
4. **Pilot evaluation** — review of pilot outcomes, curriculum effectiveness, and iteration recommendations
5. **Localization standards** — minimum requirements for adapting curriculum to a new community context

The Council does **not** have authority over:

- Community-level governance decisions (stewards and elders retain veto power — Standing Order #11)
- Partnership negotiations (program leadership)
- Budget and funding (program leadership + fiscal sponsor)
- Technology platform choices (technical team, informed by Council input)

## Composition

The Council is composed of 7-11 members drawn from five constituencies:

| Constituency | Seats | Rationale |
|-------------|-------|-----------|
| **Impact-driven AI developers** | 2-3 | Technical curriculum accuracy, aDNA standard expertise, agent safety patterns |
| **Community organizers** | 2-3 | Participatory design expertise, community accountability, cultural responsiveness |
| **Medical and public health experts** | 1-2 | Health use case accuracy, elder care domain knowledge, social determinants of health |
| **Educators** | 1-2 | Pedagogy, youth development, bilingual/multilingual education, assessment design |
| **Elder and disability advocates** | 1 | Accessibility, elder perspective, disability inclusion, administrative burden awareness |

### Membership Requirements

- **Commitment:** Minimum 4 hours/month for Council work (meetings, review, async input)
- **Term:** 1-year renewable terms; staggered to ensure continuity
- **Conflict of interest:** Members must disclose any commercial relationships with technology partners. No member may hold a financial interest that conflicts with community governance principles.
- **Community representation:** At least 2 Council members must have direct lived experience in the pilot communities or communities similar in demographics and challenges.

### Selection Process

1. Initial Council (Year 1): Appointed by program founders based on expertise and community connection
2. Subsequent terms: Nominated by existing Council members or community stewards; approved by simple majority
3. Youth observer seat: Each pilot site may designate one youth builder (age 16+) as a non-voting observer to Council meetings — ensuring youth voice without burdening young participants with governance overhead

## Decision-Making

- **Curriculum changes:** Consensus preferred; simple majority (>50%) if consensus is not reached after one meeting cycle
- **Ethical guardrails:** Supermajority (>66%) required for establishing or modifying guardrails
- **Emergency decisions:** Any single Council member may temporarily suspend a curriculum module or agent deployment pending full Council review (safety valve)
- **Community veto:** Per Standing Order #11, community stewards and elders retain veto power over use cases. The Council cannot override a community's decision to reject a curriculum element or use case.

## Meeting Cadence

| Meeting Type | Frequency | Format | Purpose |
|-------------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Full Council | Monthly | Video call (90 min) | Curriculum review, milestone assessment, policy decisions |
| Working groups | Bi-weekly | Async + short sync | Track-specific curriculum development (Builder, Steward, Elder) |
| Pilot review | Quarterly | Video call (120 min) | Evaluate pilot outcomes, iterate curriculum, plan next phase |
| Emergency session | As needed | Video call (60 min) | Safety concerns, urgent curriculum or ethical issues |

## Relationship to Pilot Communities

The Council serves the communities, not the other way around. Key principles:

1. **Pilots are sovereign** (Standing Order #9). Grand Rapids is not LA. The Council provides the framework; communities adapt it.
2. **Stewards have a direct line.** Community stewards may raise concerns to the Council at any time, not only during scheduled reviews.
3. **Curriculum is a context graph** (Standing Order #8). The Council maintains the master curriculum graph; pilot communities fork and localize it, feeding improvements back upstream.
4. **No curriculum without community.** New modules require at least one community steward to review and approve before deployment to any pilot site.

## Deliverables

The Council is responsible for producing and maintaining:

- **Curriculum framework document** — track structures, module sequences, competency milestones
- **Ethical guardrails document** — what agents should/shouldn't do, safety boundaries, escalation procedures
- **Evaluation rubrics** — per-track assessment criteria for participant progress
- **Pilot review reports** — quarterly assessment of each pilot site's curriculum effectiveness
- **Localization guidelines** — standards for adapting curriculum to new communities

## Accountability

- Council meeting minutes are shared with all community stewards
- Annual self-assessment: Council reviews its own effectiveness and composition
- Community feedback: Each pilot site conducts an annual curriculum satisfaction survey (stewards, builders, elders)
- The Council operates in the open — no decisions behind closed doors that affect community governance
