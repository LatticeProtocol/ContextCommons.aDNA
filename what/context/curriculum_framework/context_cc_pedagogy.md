# Curriculum Framework: Pedagogy

Teaching philosophy and facilitation approach for the Context Commons program. This file defines *how* the program teaches — not what it teaches.

## Three Pedagogical Pillars

**1. Critical Pedagogy (Freire, 1970)**
Participants are co-constructors of knowledge, not passive recipients. The "banking model" — where instructors deposit knowledge into empty students — is explicitly rejected. Youth builders define problems alongside elders. Stewards shape governance alongside mentors. Education is a practice of freedom, not a transfer of information.

**2. Experiential Learning (Kolb, 1984)**
Every session cycles through four phases: concrete experience (do something), reflective observation (discuss what happened), abstract conceptualization (connect to principles), and active experimentation (try again with new understanding). Builders learn by building. Stewards learn by stewarding. Elders learn by co-designing systems that serve them.

**3. Communities of Practice (Lave & Wenger, 1991)**
Learning happens through legitimate participation in real community work, not through simulation. Core Track participants begin at the periphery — observing, asking questions, building small personal context graphs. Builder Track participants move toward full participation — deploying working systems for real community members. The transition is gradual, scaffolded, and supported by mentors and stewards.

## Intergenerational Facilitation

The Context Commons is unusual: youth teach elders, elders teach youth, and stewards bridge both. Each dynamic requires distinct facilitation:

| Dynamic | Facilitation Approach | Key Principle |
|---------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Youth → Elders** | The elder is the domain expert; the youth is the tool expert. Start with the elder's problem, not the technology. Patience, dignity, no condescension (Standing Order #10). | Mutual respect across asymmetric expertise |
| **Elders → Youth** | Elders share lived experience, institutional memory, community context. Youth listen first, then build. Storytelling is a valid input format. | Wisdom is data the graph needs |
| **Stewards ↔ Both** | Stewards translate between technical and community registers. They model governance in real time. When a mentor and elder disagree, the steward facilitates — not the curriculum. | Governance is practiced, not taught |
| **Mentors → Stewards** | Mentors provide technical scaffolding within the steward's zone of proximal development (Vygotsky, 1978). Stewards don't need to become developers — they need to become informed governors. | Scaffold, don't supplant |

## Facilitation Approaches by Track

| Track | Primary Method | Why This Method |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Core** | Discussion-based, hands-on activities | Low barrier; community-relevant examples; builds shared vocabulary |
| **Builder** | Project-based, pair programming, code review | Learning through making; peer feedback normalizes iteration |
| **Steward** | Case study, practicum observation, governance simulation | Real-world judgment requires real-world scenarios (Ostrom, 1990) |
| **Elder** | Demonstration-first, storytelling, co-design workshops | Start with what helps *them*; technology follows need, not the reverse |

## Language and Accessibility

- **Community-facing content**: 8th-grade reading level maximum. Technical terms defined on first use. Jargon is a barrier, not a credential.
- **Multilingual delivery**: First-class design constraint. LA pilot operates across 100+ languages; at minimum, materials in English and Spanish. Voice-first interaction modes for participants with low vision or limited literacy.
- **Offline-capable**: All materials function without continuous internet. Grand Rapids and LA both have connectivity gaps. Unreliable internet is assumed, not edge-cased (hooks, 1994 — design for the margins).
- **Accessibility pathways**: Large-text options, screen-reader compatibility, extended time accommodations, oral demonstration alternatives to written reflection.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Deficit framing** | "Underserved communities need our help" | Centers the helper, not the community. Communities have assets; they lack specific tools. |
| **Technology solutionism** | "AI will fix healthcare / immigration / education" | AI is a tool, not a solution. Community governance determines outcomes, not software. |
| **Banking model** | "We teach, you learn" | Destroys the intergenerational loop. Every participant has expertise the program needs. |
| **Extractive research** | "We'll study your community to improve our product" | Violates non-negotiables #1 and #2. Community context belongs to the community. |
