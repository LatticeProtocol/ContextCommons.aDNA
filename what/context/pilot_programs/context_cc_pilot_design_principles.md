# Pilot Design Principles

## Design Philosophy

A Context Commons pilot is not a program delivery — it is a partnership. The pilot does not arrive in a community; it is co-created with a community. Every design decision, from session scheduling to hardware procurement to evaluation criteria, must pass through the community's governance structure before it becomes operational. If the community does not recognize itself in the pilot plan, the plan is wrong.

These principles are structural commitments, not aspirations. They constrain what a pilot can look like, how it operates, and how it ends. A pilot that violates any of these principles is not a Context Commons pilot, regardless of what it is called.

## Seven Principles

### 1. Pilot Sovereignty

Each pilot community designs its own implementation. The Context Commons provides a framework — curriculum tracks, mentorship model, hardware specifications, evaluation methods — but the community decides how that framework manifests locally. Grand Rapids is not Los Angeles. A church-based concentrated model is not a multi-institutional distributed model. Neither is better; both are valid. Adaptation is a first-class design concern, not a compromise.

**In practice**: Every pilot plan includes a localization section specifying how the community has adapted scheduling, language, examples, use case domains, and governance to its own context. The master curriculum framework is a fork point, not a mandate. Communities control localization patches; the Curriculum Council controls structural changes to the master (Standing Order #9).

### 2. Community Governance Over Operations

Stewards and elders govern all operational decisions within their pilot. This is not a consultative role — it is decision-making authority. A Lattice mentor may advise on technical trade-offs; the community steward decides what the community builds. A technology partner may offer resources; the community decides whether and how to accept them. Veto power is structural: a steward or elder veto is final and not subject to appeal by mentors, partners, or the Curriculum Council (Standing Order #11).

**In practice**: Every pilot has named steward(s) with documented governance authority. The Community Engagement Agreement specifies that operational decisions within the pilot are the steward's domain. Dispute resolution follows the three-step escalation (direct conversation → steward mediation → Council arbitration), but community governance questions are resolved by the steward, not the Council.

### 3. Hardware and Logistics as Design Constraints

Budget, internet connectivity, physical space, device availability, and scheduling are not implementation details. They are design constraints that shape what is possible and what must be adapted. A pilot that assumes reliable broadband when the community has intermittent connectivity is not well-designed. A session schedule that conflicts with the church calendar or school year is not well-designed. Logistics are addressed first, not last (Standing Order #12).

**In practice**: Every pilot plan includes a logistics section with hardware inventory, connectivity assessment (tested during actual session times, not peak hours), space capacity, scheduling calendar, and budget estimate. The pre-launch checklist verifies logistics before the first session. Unresolved logistics gaps pause the pilot — they do not become problems for stewards to absorb.

### 4. Evaluation as Learning

Pilot evaluation exists to help the community and the program learn, not to judge performance. Evaluation is developmental (Patton, 2011) — it adapts as the pilot evolves, surfaces emergent patterns, and feeds findings back into operations in real time. It is not summative assessment imposed at the end. Communities define what success means to them alongside the program's quantitative metrics. An evaluation framework that the community does not understand or endorse is not valid.

**In practice**: Every pilot begins with a collaborative evaluation design session where stewards, mentors, and (where appropriate) youth builders define community success criteria. These criteria sit alongside program metrics (completion rates, capstone quality, hardware utilization) but are not subordinate to them. Quarterly community reviews present findings in plain language and ask the community what should change.

### 5. Intergenerational Loop Requirement

Every pilot must demonstrate bidirectional knowledge flow across generations. Elders contribute context and use cases; youth build solutions; stewards govern the process; all three teach each other. A pilot where youth build systems that elders never use is incomplete. A pilot where elders provide needs but never see results is extractive. The circular knowledge economy described in the program thesis is not a metaphor — it is a testable design requirement.

**In practice**: Every pilot plan specifies how the Elder & Community Member Track intersects with the Builder Track — co-design sessions, capstone demonstrations, ongoing access support. Evaluation includes intergenerational interaction metrics: number of co-design sessions held, elder participation in capstone evaluation, youth-to-elder teaching sessions, elder-reported usefulness of deployed systems.

### 6. Exit Dignity

A pilot can end — for any reason — and the community keeps everything. Every system built, every context graph created, every piece of hardware earned belongs to the community. The program provides 90-day transition support after exit. No data is extracted. No systems are disabled. No relationships are severed. Exit is not failure; it is a possible and dignified outcome.

**In practice**: The Community Engagement Agreement includes explicit exit terms. Either party may exit with 30 days' written notice. Community-built systems must never depend on program-controlled infrastructure that cannot be transferred. Open standards (aDNA, Lattice Protocol) ensure portability. Hardware earned through completion is unconditional — it is not reclaimed on exit.

### 7. Adaptability-First Design

No pilot plan survives first contact with a community unchanged. The pilot design must accommodate revision without structural failure. Session schedules shift. Cohort sizes change. Use cases evolve as needs assessment deepens. A rigid plan that cannot adapt is a brittle plan. Adaptability is not a reaction to problems — it is a design feature built in from the start.

**In practice**: Pilot plans include explicit adaptation mechanisms. Curriculum localization is community-controlled. Session schedules include buffer weeks. Cohort size ranges (not fixed numbers) accommodate recruitment variability. Quarterly reviews are structured decision points where the plan can be revised based on what the community is learning. The OODA cascade (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act) operates at session, mission, and campaign levels, propagating adaptations upward and restructuring downward.

## Principle Interactions

These principles are not independent — they reinforce and constrain each other:

| Interaction | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Sovereignty + Governance | Communities adapt the framework AND govern its local operation — double insulation against external override |
| Logistics + Adaptability | Logistics constraints discovered during implementation trigger plan adaptation, not blame |
| Evaluation + Intergenerational Loop | Evaluation measures whether the loop is actually working, not just whether content was delivered |
| Exit Dignity + Governance | Community governance persists even after exit — the program does not take governance with it |
| Sovereignty + Evaluation | Each community defines its own success criteria — evaluation is localized, not standardized |

## Sources

- **Patton, M.Q. (2011). *Developmental Evaluation: Applying Complexity Concepts to Enhance Innovation and Use*.** — evaluation as learning, not judgment; adaptive evaluation for emergent programs; shaped Principles 4 and 7
- **Ostrom, E. (1990). *Governing the Commons*.** — community governance of shared resources; clear boundaries, collective-choice arrangements, graduated sanctions; shaped Principles 1 and 2
- **Minkler, M. & Wallerstein, N. (2008). *Community-Based Participatory Research for Health*.** — community as co-designer, not subject; power-sharing in program design; shaped all 7 principles
- **Trickett, E.J. (2009). "Community Psychology: Individuals and Interventions in Community Context." *Annual Review of Psychology*, 60.** — ecological validity of program design; programs must fit real community conditions; shaped Principle 3
- **aDNA Context Graph Standard v2.2** — data sovereignty and open standards as technical guarantees for Principles 6 and 1
