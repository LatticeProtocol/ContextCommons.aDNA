# Pilot Evaluation Framework

## Evaluation Philosophy

Context Commons pilot evaluation is **developmental**, not summative. The distinction matters: summative evaluation asks "did the program work?" after it ends. Developmental evaluation asks "what is the program learning?" while it is running, and feeds those findings back into operations in real time. In a program designed for adaptation (Principle 7), evaluation is a steering mechanism, not a report card.

This approach draws on Patton's developmental evaluation framework: evaluation embedded in program operations, responsive to emergent complexity, and oriented toward use by the people doing the work — stewards, mentors, and builders — not just funders and researchers. Evaluation findings belong to the community first. Reports to funders and academic partners are derived from community-facing evaluation, not the other way around.

Communities define what success means to them. The program defines what it needs to measure for accountability and learning. Both are valid. Neither overrides the other. An evaluation framework the community does not understand or endorse is not valid (Pilot Design Principle 4).

## Evaluation Domains

Evaluation is organized into three domains, each with distinct audiences and methods:

### Domain 1: Program Metrics (Accountability)

Quantitative indicators the program tracks across all pilots for accountability, pattern detection, and funder reporting.

| Metric | Definition | Collection Method | Frequency | Target |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| **Track completion rate** | % of enrolled participants completing their track | Attendance records + milestone assessments | Per-cohort | ≥70% for Builder/Steward; participation-based for Elder |
| **Session attendance** | Average % attendance across sessions | Session sign-in | Weekly | ≥80% for Builder; ≥70% for Steward; ≥60% for Elder |
| **Milestone passage** | % of participants passing each competency milestone on schedule | Mentor/steward assessments per [[context_cc_competency_milestones]] | At milestone points | ≥80% passage at each gate |
| **Capstone completion** | % of Builder Track participants delivering a passing capstone | Three-reviewer evaluation per [[context_cc_assessment_framework]] | End of Builder Track | ≥85% of those who reach capstone phase |
| **Workstation distribution** | Number of MacBook Neos earned and distributed | Hardware tracking log | End of Builder Track | ≥90% of capstone completers |
| **Hardware utilization** | Hours/week of workstation and shared equipment use | System telemetry (local only, no cloud reporting) | Monthly | Increasing trend through pilot |
| **System uptime** | % of time community-deployed agent systems are operational | Automated monitoring (local) | Continuous | ≥95% during active pilot |
| **Intergenerational sessions** | Number of co-design or cross-track sessions held | Session records | Monthly | ≥2 per month during active pilot |

### Domain 2: Community-Defined Success (Sovereignty)

Each pilot community defines its own success criteria during a collaborative evaluation design session at pilot launch. These criteria reflect what the community cares about — not what the program assumes they should care about.

**Process for defining community criteria**:

1. **Evaluation design session** (pre-pilot, 2-3 hours) — Stewards, mentors, and community representatives answer: "What would it look like if this program worked for your community? What would be different?" Responses are captured in the community's own language.
2. **Criteria refinement** — Program staff help translate responses into observable/measurable criteria without changing their meaning. Community reviews and approves the final list.
3. **Quarterly check** — Community reviews criteria and updates them if priorities have shifted. New criteria can be added; outdated criteria can be retired.

**Example community criteria** (illustrative, not prescriptive):

| Community | Possible Criteria | Why It Matters to Them |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| Peter's Church (Grand Rapids) | "Our elders can get help with Medicare paperwork without asking their children to take a day off work" | Elder independence and reduced caregiver burden — a specific, lived pain point |
| Peter's Church (Grand Rapids) | "Young people from our church can explain what they do to their parents in a way that makes their parents proud" | Community legibility of the program — parents must see value, not just "computer stuff" |
| LAUSD / Venice Beach (LA) | "Families can get immigration form help in Spanish without waiting in a 3-hour line at the legal aid office" | Concrete time and access savings for a specific population |
| LAUSD / Venice Beach (LA) | "The Venice Beach Book Club has a tech program that attracts teenagers, not just retirees" | Intergenerational bridge — the Book Club's own sustainability goal |

### Domain 3: Research Outcomes (Knowledge)

For pilots with academic partners (GVSU for Grand Rapids, UCLA for Los Angeles), research evaluation produces publishable knowledge about community-governed AI education. This domain is secondary to Domains 1 and 2 — research serves the program and community, not the reverse.

| Research Question | Method | Data Source | Ethics Requirement |
|------------------|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Does community governance improve AI adoption outcomes vs. top-down models? | Mixed methods — completion rates + steward interviews | Program metrics + qualitative interviews | IRB approval + community consent |
| What is the effect of intergenerational co-design on elder technology confidence? | Pre/post survey + observational coding | Elder Track participants | IRB approval + community consent; survey in participants' primary language |
| How do localization patterns differ between concentrated and distributed pilot models? | Comparative case study | Pilot A vs. Pilot B localization logs, session recordings | IRB approval + community consent; no identifying data without explicit permission |
| What competencies do youth builders develop that transfer beyond the program? | Longitudinal follow-up (6 months post-pilot) | Builder self-report + employer/educator feedback | IRB approval + individual consent; voluntary participation |

## Data Collection Methods

| Method | What It Captures | Who Collects | Frequency | Storage |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|-----------|---------|
| **Session attendance** | Participation patterns, engagement trends | Steward or session facilitator | Every session | Local session log (community-owned) |
| **Milestone assessments** | Competency progression | Mentor (technical) + steward (governance) | At milestone points | Local assessment records |
| **Capstone evaluation** | Technical quality, governance compliance, community value | Three-reviewer panel | End of Builder Track | Local evaluation rubric |
| **Steward check-ins** | Operational health, community satisfaction, emerging concerns | Program staff + steward | Monthly | Session notes (community-owned) |
| **Quarterly community review** | Community-defined success progress, plan adaptations | Community + program staff | Quarterly | Review summary document |
| **System telemetry** | Hardware and agent system utilization | Automated (local monitoring) | Continuous | Local logs only — no cloud reporting |
| **Qualitative interviews** | Deep understanding of participant experience | Academic partner researchers | Per research design (IRB-governed) | De-identified transcripts per IRB protocol |

## Reporting Cadence

| Report | Audience | Frequency | Content | Format |
|--------|---------|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Session summary** | Mentor + steward | After each session | Attendance, key moments, concerns, next session prep | Brief notes (1 page) |
| **Monthly internal** | Program leadership | Monthly | Program metrics dashboard, emerging patterns, resource needs | Structured report |
| **Quarterly community review** | Community (stewards, elders, participants) | Quarterly | Community success criteria progress, program metrics in plain language, proposed adaptations | In-person presentation + one-page summary (large print available; interpreted into community languages as needed) |
| **Funder report** | Grant funders, fiscal sponsor | Per grant terms (typically semi-annual or annual) | Outputs, outcomes, budget utilization, narrative | Per funder template |
| **Research publication** | Academic community | Per research timeline | Peer-reviewed findings per IRB-approved protocol | Journal article or conference paper |
| **Pilot close-out** | All stakeholders | End of pilot cycle | Comprehensive evaluation, AAR, recommendations for next cycle | Full report + community summary |

## Iteration Methodology

Evaluation findings feed back into pilot operations through the OODA cascade:

| Level | Trigger | Action | Example |
|-------|---------|--------|---------|
| **Session** | Session summary flags concern | Mentor/steward adjust next session | Attendance drop → steward outreach to absent participants |
| **Monthly** | Monthly metrics show pattern | Program staff + steward review; adapt plan | Builder milestone passage rate below target → add supplemental sessions |
| **Quarterly** | Quarterly review surfaces community concern | Formal plan revision; community approves changes | Community redefines success criteria based on lived experience |
| **Pilot cycle** | Close-out evaluation identifies structural issues | Campaign-level revision; Curriculum Council review | Track structure timing doesn't fit school calendar → restructure for next cohort |

## Research Ethics

All evaluation activities involving human participants are governed by:

| Requirement | Scope | Enforcement |
|------------|-------|------------|
| **Community consent** | All data collection within a pilot community requires steward approval | Steward signs data collection authorization as part of Community Engagement Agreement |
| **Individual consent** | Qualitative interviews, surveys, and any individually identifiable data require individual informed consent | Standard consent forms in participant's primary language; right to withdraw at any time |
| **IRB approval** | All research activities by academic partners (GVSU, UCLA) | Academic partner's institutional IRB; protocol reviewed by program staff for alignment with community governance |
| **Data sovereignty** | All data generated within a pilot belongs to the community | Per Community Engagement Agreement data sovereignty clause; academic access only with community consent |
| **De-identification** | Published findings must not identify individuals without explicit written consent | Standard de-identification per IRB protocol; community reviews any publication mentioning the community by name |
| **No extraction** | Evaluation data is not used for AI model training, product development, or purposes outside the program | Three non-negotiables (governance model); technical enforcement through local-only data storage |
| **Youth protections** | Participants under 18 require parental/guardian consent for research activities | Parental consent forms; youth assent forms; age-appropriate consent language |
