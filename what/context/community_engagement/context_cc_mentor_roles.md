# Mentor Roles

## Mentorship Philosophy

The Context Commons three-tier mentorship model is a network of complementary expertise, not a hierarchy. Lattice Mentors bring technical depth in context engineering and agent design. Community Stewards bring relational trust, governance authority, and local knowledge. Youth Builders bring energy, capability, and the lived experience of the communities being served. Each role teaches the others: mentors learn community context from stewards, stewards learn technical foundations from mentors, and youth builders — treated as peers, not students (Standing Order #10) — teach elders while learning from everyone.

This structure draws on Lave and Wenger's concept of legitimate peripheral participation: learning happens through guided movement from the periphery of a community of practice toward full participation. Youth builders do not study agentic systems in the abstract — they build real systems for real people, supported by mentors and stewards who are themselves learning and adapting. The mentorship relationship is bidirectional by design and intergenerational by necessity.

## Three-Tier Role Comparison

| Dimension | Lattice Mentor | Community Steward | Youth Builder (Peer Mentor) |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| **Who** | Developer from aDNA/Lattice community, partner engineer, or university CS faculty/student | Local leader — pastor, teacher, organizer, health worker, community elder | Young person (14-24) who has completed or is advanced in Builder Track |
| **Primary function** | Teach technical foundations; support builders on context engineering, agent design, and deployment | Bridge between technical mentors and community; govern local priorities; co-facilitate sessions | Build agent systems for community needs; teach elders; mentor newer builders |
| **Time commitment** | 4-6 hours/week during active pilot (16 weeks) | 6-8 hours/week during active pilot; 2-4 hours/week ongoing | Full program (~100 hours total); peer mentoring 1-2 hours/week in later phases |
| **Minimum term** | One pilot cycle (16 weeks); renewal by mutual agreement | Ongoing — community role, not time-limited | Evolves from participant to peer mentor after capstone |
| **Training required** | Mentor onboarding (program values, community context, pedagogy basics, anti-patterns) | Steward Track completion (8 weeks, ~40 hours) | Core Track + Builder Track; peer mentoring orientation |
| **Assessed by** | Curriculum Council review + community steward feedback | Community peers + Lattice mentor partnership feedback | Mentor + steward (capstone evaluation rubric) |
| **Compensated** | Stipend or honorarium (budget Phase 4); partner-employed mentors may volunteer through corporate programs | Honorarium for pilot participation (budget Phase 4); role is community-embedded, not program-employed | MacBook Neo earned through completion; peer mentoring stipend or service credit (budget Phase 4) |

## Lattice Mentor Profile

### Who Becomes a Lattice Mentor

Lattice Mentors are recruited from the aDNA/Lattice developer community, technology partner organizations (Anthropic engineers, Google developers), university computer science departments, and the broader open-source AI community. They are technical practitioners who also care about community impact — both qualities are required, and neither substitutes for the other.

### Prerequisites

| Prerequisite | Why It Matters |
|-------------|---------------|
| Working knowledge of aDNA Context Graph Standard | Mentors teach context engineering — they must be fluent practitioners |
| Claude Code proficiency | The primary development environment for youth builders |
| Empathy and patience for non-technical learners | Stewards and youth start from varied technical baselines; a brilliant engineer who cannot teach at the learner's level is not a fit |
| Cultural humility | Mentors enter communities where they may be outsiders; they must listen before advising and respect community governance even when they disagree on technical grounds |

### Responsibilities

| Responsibility | Frequency | Context |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Lead/co-facilitate Builder Track sessions | 2× weekly during pilot | Technical instruction; co-facilitated with steward for community context |
| Review youth builder context graphs | Weekly | Formative feedback on context engineering quality, structure, and documentation |
| Evaluate competency milestones B1-B4 | At milestone points | Technical evaluation per competency rubric in [[what/context/curriculum_framework/context_cc_competency_milestones]] |
| Support stewards on technical foundations | Weekly during Steward Track | Build steward's technical literacy for governance; answer questions; co-design tools |
| Guide capstone projects | Weeks 9-16 | Technical guidance and quality assurance; do not build for the builder |
| Participate in Curriculum Council working groups | Bi-weekly (async acceptable) | Curriculum development, iteration based on pilot experience, technical standards |
| Document lessons learned | End of each pilot cycle | Contribute to AAR and curriculum revision process |

### Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Correction |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Taking over a builder's project** | Undermines agency; the builder learns nothing; the community gets a system no one local can maintain | Guide, review, suggest — never write the builder's code |
| **Overriding steward governance decisions** | Violates Standing Order #11; destroys trust; community governance is not a suggestion | Technical advice is always welcome; community governance decisions are always final |
| **Teaching tools instead of thinking** | Produces users, not builders; tools change, thinking persists | Emphasize context engineering principles and problem decomposition over specific tool syntax |
| **Deficit framing** | Positions community as lacking rather than possessing different knowledge | The community has knowledge the mentor does not; the exchange is mutual |

## Community Steward Profile

### Who Becomes a Steward

Stewards are not appointed from outside. They emerge from existing community leadership — the pastor who coordinates elder care, the teacher who runs after-school programs, the organizer who knows every family in the neighborhood. Stewardship is a formalization of trust that already exists, not a role imposed by the program.

### Responsibilities

| Responsibility | Frequency | Context |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Govern community engagement | Ongoing | Represent community interests in all program decisions; enforce non-negotiables locally |
| Co-facilitate sessions | During pilot | Bridge between mentor's technical content and community context; translate concepts |
| Conduct needs assessment | Pre-pilot + quarterly | Identify community needs that become capstone project sources; update as needs evolve |
| Evaluate governance milestones | At milestone points | Ensure builders respect community data rules and governance boundaries |
| Mediate disputes | As needed | Per dispute resolution framework: direct conversation first, then Council if unresolved |
| Maintain community systems post-pilot | Ongoing | Sustainability: ensure agent systems continue serving the community after pilot ends |
| Report to community governance body | Monthly | Keep church board, school administration, or community council informed |

### Veto Power

Per Standing Order #11, community stewards and elders have veto power over use cases and deployments within their community. This power is:

- **Structural, not advisory** — a veto is final, not a recommendation to be weighed
- **Not subject to override** — neither mentors, partners, nor the Curriculum Council can force reconsideration
- **Exercised at the community's initiative** — a community may reconsider a veto on its own terms, at its own pace
- **Infrequently needed** — collaborative design processes should surface concerns before they reach veto; a veto usually indicates a process failure upstream

## Youth Builder as Peer Mentor

After completing the Builder Track and their capstone project, advanced youth builders may take on peer mentoring roles with newer cohorts. This is the circular knowledge economy in action — yesterday's learner becomes tomorrow's mentor.

Peer mentoring is:
- **Voluntary** — no builder is required to mentor; it is offered, not assigned
- **Supported** — peer mentors receive orientation and ongoing guidance from Lattice Mentors and stewards
- **Compensated** — stipend or community service credit (budget in Phase 4 / fundraising)
- **Bounded** — 1-2 hours/week; peer mentors are not responsible for session planning or curriculum delivery
- **Peer-to-peer** — per Standing Order #10, the mentoring relationship is collaborative, not hierarchical

## Mentor Recruitment and Onboarding

| Step | Activity | Who | Duration |
|------|---------|-----|----------|
| **1. Identify candidates** | Source from aDNA community, partner organizations, universities, Curriculum Council networks | Program leadership + partner organizations | Ongoing |
| **2. Screening conversation** | Assess technical skills, cultural humility, teaching aptitude, commitment capacity | Program leadership + prospective mentor | 30-60 minutes |
| **3. Mentor onboarding** | Program values, three non-negotiables, community context briefing, pedagogy orientation, anti-pattern review | Program leadership (facilitated session) | 3-4 hours |
| **4. Community introduction** | In-person visit to pilot site; meet steward(s) and community members; relationship building | Steward + new mentor | Half-day, on-site |
| **5. Shadow sessions** | New mentor observes experienced mentor leading 1-2 sessions; debrief after each | Experienced mentor + new mentor | 1-2 sessions |
| **6. Active mentoring** | Mentor begins co-leading sessions with steward; independent leadership after 2-3 co-led sessions | New mentor + steward | Ongoing |

## Mentor Support and Sustainability

| Support Structure | Description |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **Mentor cohort** | Peer support network among all active mentors across pilot sites; shared channel, bi-weekly sync |
| **Curriculum Council access** | Mentors participate in relevant Council working groups; voice in curriculum development |
| **Steward partnership** | Each mentor is paired with a community steward; the steward provides community context, the mentor provides technical support |
| **Burnout prevention** | Clear time commitments (4-6 hours/week cap); explicit scope boundaries; no scope creep without renegotiation |
| **Professional development** | Mentors gain community engagement experience, pedagogy skills, cross-cultural fluency — documented for career advancement |
| **Stipend/honorarium** | Budget allocation in Phase 4 / fundraising; partner-employed mentors may contribute through corporate volunteer programs |

## Sources

- **Lave, J. & Wenger, E. (1991). *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*.** — the three-tier model as a community of practice; mentorship as guided movement from periphery to full participation
- **Mezirow, J. (2000). *Learning as Transformation*.** — transformative learning theory; mentors must be prepared for their own transformation through community engagement, not only the learner's
- **Daloz, L.A. (1999). *Mentor: Guiding the Journey of Adult Learners*.** — effective mentoring requires support structures for the mentors themselves; mentoring is a relationship, not a role
- **Eby, L.T. et al. (2008). "Does Mentoring Matter? A Multidisciplinary Meta-Analysis." *Journal of Vocational Behavior*, 72(2).** — evidence for structured mentoring programs; formal programs with training, matching, and ongoing support outperform informal arrangements
- **aDNA Context Graph Standard v2.2** — context engineering as the technical skill mentors teach; the standard itself models the mentorship content
