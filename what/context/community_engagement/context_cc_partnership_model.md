# Partnership Model

## Partnership Philosophy

Context Commons partnerships are mutual engagements, not transactions. Neither party is a vendor; neither is a customer. Every partnership is governed by the program's three non-negotiables: community governance is non-negotiable, no data extraction, and open standards. Partners contribute resources — hardware, compute, mentorship, space, expertise — while communities contribute governance, participation, trust, and local knowledge. The exchange is symmetrical in value even when asymmetrical in kind.

This philosophy draws on Ostrom's design principles for durable commons institutions: clearly defined boundaries (who is a partner and what the partnership covers), collective-choice arrangements (communities participate in decisions about how partner resources are used), and graduated sanctions (the partner violation protocol escalates proportionally). Partnerships that cannot operate within these principles do not proceed — not because they are unwelcome, but because the program's integrity depends on structural guarantees, not goodwill alone.

## Partnership Types

| Type | Examples | What They Contribute | What We Offer | Governance |
|------|----------|---------------------|---------------|------------|
| **Technology** | Anthropic, Apple, Google | Hardware grants, API access, compute, technical mentorship, distribution | Impact stories, edge deployment feedback, responsible AI model, youth pipeline | Three non-negotiables apply; partner violation protocol governs breaches |
| **Academic** | UCLA, GVSU | Research collaboration, evaluation framework, student mentors, grant co-PI | Longitudinal data access (community-governed), co-authorship, field sites | IRB approval + community consent required; data sovereignty preserved |
| **Community** | Peter's Church, LAUSD, Venice Beach Book Club | Physical space, participants, local knowledge, steward leadership, governance | Curriculum, hardware, mentorship, agent systems, ongoing technical support | Community sovereignty; steward/elder veto is final (Standing Order #11) |
| **Fiscal** | Fiscal sponsor (Model C recommended) | Legal/financial infrastructure, grant eligibility, tax-deductible donations, compliance | Program operations, financial accountability, mission execution | Financial oversight per Model C terms; operational autonomy preserved |

## Engagement Lifecycle

Every partnership follows a five-phase lifecycle. Phases are sequential — no phase is skipped — and each has explicit exit criteria that must be met before advancing.

| Phase | Activities | Duration | Exit Criteria | Who Leads |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Discovery** | Initial outreach, alignment conversation, non-negotiable review, mutual interest check | 2-4 weeks | Both parties confirm alignment with three non-negotiables; mutual interest documented | Program leadership |
| **Scoping** | Define contribution type, scale, timeline; draft partnership terms; steward consultation (for community partnerships) | 4-8 weeks | Written scope document; partner and steward sign-off; no unresolved non-negotiable concerns | Program leadership + partner contact |
| **Onboarding** | Integration into program operations; first contributions delivered; communication channels established; community introduction (if applicable) | 4-6 weeks | First resource delivered; communication norms agreed; partner assigned to specific pilot or workstream | Steward (community) or program lead (tech/academic) |
| **Active** | Ongoing contributions; quarterly review cycles; participation in relevant Council working groups | Ongoing | Quarterly review confirms continued alignment and mutual value; contributions on track | Joint (program + partner) |
| **Renewal / Exit** | Annual comprehensive review; decision to continue, modify, or conclude; transition plan if exiting | Annual | Partnership renewed with updated terms, modified to reflect changed circumstances, or concluded gracefully with transition complete | Program leadership |

**Exit is not failure.** Partnerships end for many reasons — strategic shifts, resource constraints, mission evolution. A graceful exit preserves the relationship and the possibility of future collaboration. Community-built systems must never depend on a single partner's continued participation; the open-standards non-negotiable ensures portability.

## Mutual Expectations by Partnership Type

### Technology Partners

| What We Ask | What We Offer |
|-------------|--------------|
| Hardware grants (MacBook Neo units, workstations) funded through education/impact programs | Authentic community impact stories demonstrating beneficial AI adoption |
| API access and compute grants for community-operated agent systems | Edge deployment feedback — real user needs, failure modes, design insights |
| Technical mentorship hours from company engineers and researchers | A model for responsible AI they can reference, learn from, and replicate |
| Distribution and visibility through corporate channels | Alignment with published commitments to equitable access |
| Respect for three non-negotiables in all interactions | Youth pipeline: graduates with real agentic systems experience |

### Academic Partners

| What We Ask | What We Offer |
|-------------|--------------|
| Research collaboration and longitudinal program evaluation | Field sites with genuine community partnerships (not extractive research) |
| Student mentors for pilot programs | Co-authorship on community-engaged research publications |
| Grant co-PI relationships (NSF, IMLS, DOL) | A replicable model for community-university partnership in AI education |
| Faculty advisory support for curriculum development | Curriculum Council participation — academic voice in program governance |

### Community Partners

| What We Ask | What We Offer |
|-------------|--------------|
| Physical space for sessions (2× weekly, 16+ weeks) | Full curriculum delivered by trained mentors |
| Identified steward(s) willing to complete Steward Track | Hardware for youth builders and community workstations |
| Youth cohort (8-16 participants, ages 14-24) | Ongoing technical support and system maintenance |
| Elder and community member participation in co-design | Agent systems built to community-identified needs, governed by community |
| Steward/elder governance over community use cases | Peer network with other pilot communities |

### Fiscal Partners

| What We Ask | What We Offer |
|-------------|--------------|
| 501(c)(3) umbrella for grant eligibility and tax-deductible donations | Clean financial reporting and program accountability |
| Financial administration and compliance support | Mission-aligned programming that strengthens the sponsor's portfolio |
| Pass-through grant management at reasonable overhead (8-12%) | Transition path to independent 501(c)(3) at $250K+ annual revenue |

## Partnership Agreement Checklist

Before formalizing any partnership, the following items must be confirmed and documented:

| Item | Description | Required For |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Non-negotiable acknowledgment | Partner has reviewed and explicitly accepted the three non-negotiables | All partnerships |
| Data handling terms | Clear statement on data ownership, access, and community sovereignty | Technology, Academic |
| Contribution scope | Specific resources, timeline, and deliverables from each party | All partnerships |
| Community introduction | Partner has met relevant community steward(s) and been introduced to pilot site | Technology, Academic |
| Review schedule | Quarterly review cadence and annual renewal date established | All partnerships |
| Exit terms | Transition plan for community-facing systems if partnership ends | Technology, Academic |
| Steward approval | Community steward(s) have approved the partnership for their community | Community (always), Technology/Academic (when community-facing) |
| IP and licensing | Confirmation that all program outputs remain under open standards | Technology, Academic |

## Governance Guardrails

Partnership governance is not restated here — the authoritative source is [[what/context/program_design/context_cc_governance_model]]. Key references:

- **Three non-negotiables** — the structural foundation for all partnership terms
- **Partner violation protocol** — five-step escalation (informal conversation → formal notice → Council review → suspension → termination)
- **Dispute resolution** — three-step process (direct conversation → steward mediation → Council arbitration)
- **Community vetoes are final** — per Standing Orders #7 and #11, no external party may override community governance decisions

What this file adds beyond the governance model: the lifecycle structure that operationalizes these principles, the partnership-type-specific expectations, and the pre-agreement checklist that ensures compliance is confirmed before commitments are made.
