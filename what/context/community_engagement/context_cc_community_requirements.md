# Community Requirements

## Readiness Philosophy

Community readiness assessment is not a gatekeeping exercise. It exists to protect communities from a program they are not yet equipped to sustain — and to protect the program from making promises it cannot keep in an unprepared setting. A community that does not yet meet readiness criteria is not rejected; it is supported toward readiness or connected with resources that can help it get there.

Readiness is assessed collaboratively with the community, never about the community from the outside. The assessment process itself builds the relationship — a community visit, face-to-face conversation, and shared checklist review are required steps, not bureaucratic formalities. If a community's stewards and elders do not endorse proceeding, the program does not proceed, regardless of what any external assessment says. This is Standing Order #11 in practice: elder and steward veto power is structural, not advisory.

## Community Readiness Requirements

Requirements are organized into hard gates (must be met before the pilot begins) and soft prerequisites (preferable but adaptable). Every requirement is tested against both pilot communities to ensure it is realistic for a concentrated church-based model (Grand Rapids) and a distributed multi-institutional model (Los Angeles).

### Hard Gates

These must be met before pilot operations begin. A deficiency in any hard gate pauses the pilot until resolved.

| Requirement | Testable Criteria | Grand Rapids | Los Angeles |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Named steward(s)** | At least 1 steward identified and committed to Steward Track (8 weeks, ~40 hours) | Pastor or church board designee — single institution simplifies identification | LAUSD teacher champion + Venice Beach facilitator + UCLA liaison — multi-institutional requires 2-3 stewards |
| **Elder/steward endorsement** | Steward(s) can articulate and endorse the three non-negotiables; community governing body has approved participation | Church board vote or pastoral endorsement — familiar governance structure | Multi-institutional MOU or coordinated letters of intent — more complex, requires parallel conversations |
| **Youth cohort** | 8-16 youth (ages 14-24) identified, interested, and available for Builder Track (12 weeks, ~80 hours, plus 4-week Core Track) | 8-12 via church youth group and neighborhood outreach — known population, existing relationships | 12-16 via LAUSD programs, Venice Beach Book Club, UCLA outreach — larger pool but more coordination to recruit |
| **Physical space** | Space for 8-16 people with tables, power outlets, projection/display capability; available 2× per week for 16+ weeks | Church hall or classrooms — single site, scheduling through church calendar | LAUSD computer lab + Venice Beach Book Club + UCLA facility — multi-site rotation, scheduling across 3 institutions |
| **Session-time connectivity** | Reliable internet during session hours; minimum 25 Mbps downstream | Municipal broadband or church Wi-Fi — verify reliability during actual session times, not just peak hours | School/campus Wi-Fi — generally reliable; verify Venice Beach Book Club connectivity specifically |
| **Steward/elder veto confirmed** | Steward(s) understand and accept that community governance decisions (including vetoes) are final and not subject to override | Straightforward — church governance tradition already operates this way | Requires explicit agreement from all 3 institutions that no single institution can override community governance consensus |

### Soft Prerequisites

Preferable but not blocking. The program adapts when these are absent.

| Prerequisite | Ideal State | Adaptation if Absent |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Personal device ownership** | Youth have personal laptops | Program provides shared workstations during sessions; MacBook Neo earned through completion |
| **Home broadband** | Participants have internet access at home | Curriculum includes offline-capable materials; key exercises designed for session-only connectivity |
| **Academic partner** | University partner for research, evaluation, grant co-PI | Program can operate without academic partner; evaluation shifts to internal metrics; grant opportunities narrow |
| **Local funding sources** | Identified local foundations or government programs | Program operates on central funding; local sources supplement but are not required |
| **Prior tech education exposure** | Youth have some coding or digital literacy background | Core Track provides foundation; Builder Track pacing adjusts; no prior experience assumed |
| **Multilingual support capacity** | Materials available in community languages | English-first launch with translation as Phase 2 priority; bilingual stewards bridge the gap |

## Community Engagement Agreement

Before a pilot begins, both parties sign a Community Engagement Agreement. The agreement is not a legal contract for services — it is a mutual commitment document. Contents:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Parties** | Community (represented by named steward(s)) and Context Commons program |
| **Non-negotiable acknowledgment** | Community has reviewed, discussed, and endorses the three non-negotiables |
| **Program commitments** | Curriculum delivery, mentor deployment, hardware provisioning, ongoing technical support, data sovereignty guarantee |
| **Community commitments** | Physical space, participant recruitment, steward participation, elder/community involvement, governance engagement |
| **Data sovereignty clause** | Explicit statement: all context graphs and data generated by the community belong to the community; the program has no ownership claim; academic partners access data only with community consent and IRB approval |
| **Timeline and milestones** | Pilot duration, cohort size targets, session schedule, milestone review dates |
| **Quarterly review** | Both parties review progress, address concerns, confirm continued alignment every 90 days |
| **Exit terms** | Either party may exit with 30 days' written notice; community retains all systems, data, and hardware; program provides 90-day transition support |
| **Dispute resolution** | Per governance model: direct conversation → steward mediation → Council arbitration |

## Readiness Assessment Process

Assessment is a collaborative, relationship-building activity — not an external evaluation imposed on the community.

| Step | Activity | Who | Duration | Output |
|------|---------|-----|----------|--------|
| **1. Initial conversation** | Mutual introduction; program explains vision and non-negotiables; community shares needs and interests | Program leadership + community contact (pastor, organizer, teacher) | 1-2 hours | Interest confirmed or declined (either outcome is fine) |
| **2. Community visit** | In-person visit to the community site; meet potential stewards, youth, elders; observe existing programs; assess space and infrastructure | Program leadership + prospective steward | Half-day, on-site (non-negotiable — no remote-only assessments) | Needs assessment notes, space evaluation, relationship foundation |
| **3. Hard gate checklist** | Walk through hard gate requirements together; identify any gaps; create a plan to address gaps (or agree the timing is not right) | Program leadership + steward(s) | 1-2 hours | Completed checklist — all hard gates met, or gap resolution plan with timeline |
| **4. Community decision** | Steward(s) present the program to community governance body (church board, school administration, etc.); community decides whether to proceed | Steward(s) + community governance body | Community's own timeline (do not rush this step) | Community agrees to proceed, or declines (no pressure, no consequences for declining) |
| **5. Agreement signed** | Community Engagement Agreement reviewed, discussed, and signed by both parties | Program leadership + steward(s) | 1-2 hours | Signed agreement; pilot preparation begins |

**Total timeline**: 6-12 weeks from initial conversation to signed agreement, depending on community governance pace. The program adjusts to the community's schedule, not the other way around.

## Ongoing Requirements

Readiness is not a one-time gate. During the pilot, the following must remain true:

| Requirement | Review Frequency | Response if Not Met |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Steward(s) actively engaged | Monthly check-in | Identify support needed; recruit additional steward(s) if necessary |
| Physical space available | Per session | Arrange backup space; adjust schedule if needed |
| Youth participation above 60% | Monthly (session attendance) | Steward outreach to participants; adjust scheduling; address barriers |
| Community governance functioning | Quarterly review | Escalate to Council if governance breaks down; pause pilot if no resolution |
| Data sovereignty maintained | Continuous (technical monitoring) | Immediate incident response per governance model protocols |

- **Fetterman, D.M. (2001). *Foundations of Empowerment Evaluation*.** — evaluation and assessment as capacity-building activities, not judgment exercises; shaped the "readiness is not gatekeeping" philosophy
