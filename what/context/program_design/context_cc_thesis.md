# The Context Commons Thesis

## The Problem in Two Parts

**Young people are being prepared for a world that no longer exists.** Agentic AI systems are restructuring how knowledge work, administration, creative production, and economic opportunity function — faster than any school system can update a curriculum. The children who will thrive are those who learn to build and direct these systems, not merely consume them. Today, that capability accrues almost exclusively to the employees and families of a handful of technology companies in a handful of cities.

**Older people are being left behind by a world that is leaving too fast.** Elder care systems are already past crisis. Administrative complexity in healthcare, immigration, housing, benefits, and civic participation grows annually while the support workforce shrinks. The elders who will suffer most are those in small communities without institutional resources — precisely the communities where intergenerational knowledge, cultural continuity, and mutual care have historically been strongest.

These are not separate problems. They are one problem with one solution: **rebuild the intergenerational knowledge loop, and arm it with the most powerful tools available.**

## The Thesis

The aDNA Context Graph is an open ontology standard for structuring human and organizational knowledge so that AI agents can act on it faithfully — not as black-box oracles, but as directed tools that respect the context, values, and priorities of the people and communities they serve.

The Context Commons program uses this standard — and the developer community building on it — to create **a teach/learn mentorship network** in which:

- **Community stewards** (organizers, pastors, teachers, elders) learn how context-driven agentic systems work and what they can do.
- **Young people** learn to build, configure, and operate these systems — earning their own hardware and real capability in the process.
- **Elders and community members** become the beneficiaries and co-designers of the systems these young people build — defining the problems, validating the solutions, and holding the context that makes the agents useful.

## The Circular Knowledge Economy

The result is a **circular knowledge economy** at the community level: elders hold context, youth hold capability, stewards hold governance, and the agentic systems they build together serve the specific, local, real needs of their community — not a product roadmap set in San Francisco.

```
    Elders (Context)
         ↕
Youth (Capability) ←→ Stewards (Governance)
         ↕
    Agent Systems
   (serve community)
```

Knowledge flows in every direction. Lattice mentors learn community context they could never access from a lab. Stewards gain technical fluency. Youth gain agency and career-grade skills. Elders gain tools and dignity. The network is not extractive — it is mutualistic.
