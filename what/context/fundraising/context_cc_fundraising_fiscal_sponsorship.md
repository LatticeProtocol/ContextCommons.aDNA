# Fiscal Sponsorship

## Why CC Needs a Fiscal Sponsor

Most grants require the applicant to be a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt organization. CC likely does not have this status initially, and obtaining it takes 3-12 months with the IRS. A fiscal sponsor is an established nonprofit that lets CC receive tax-deductible donations and apply for grants through the sponsor's tax-exempt status — essentially borrowing their nonprofit infrastructure while CC builds its own.

This is not a workaround. Fiscal sponsorship is a standard, well-understood mechanism used by thousands of programs. Many funders explicitly accept fiscally sponsored projects.

## Two Models

### Model A — Comprehensive (Sponsor Owns the Project)

The fiscal sponsor legally owns CC as a program within its organization. The sponsor provides full back-office support: HR, payroll, insurance, accounting, legal compliance. CC operates as a program, not an independent entity.

**Pros**: Maximum support. The sponsor handles compliance, payroll taxes, liability insurance. CC can focus entirely on program delivery.
**Cons**: Minimum autonomy. The sponsor has legal authority over the project. Hiring, spending, and strategy decisions may need sponsor approval.
**Best for**: Brand-new programs that need full infrastructure and have not yet built operational capacity.

### Model C — Pre-Approved Grant Relationship

CC remains a separate entity (or individual). The fiscal sponsor receives grants on CC's behalf and re-grants the funds. CC maintains its own books, hires its own staff, and makes its own programmatic decisions.

**Pros**: More autonomy. CC controls its operations and builds its own organizational capacity from day one.
**Cons**: CC must maintain its own accounting, HR, and compliance systems. The sponsor provides a tax-exempt umbrella but less operational support.
**Best for**: Projects that plan to eventually spin out as independent 501(c)(3) organizations and want to build capacity early.

## Selection Criteria

When evaluating fiscal sponsors, assess:

1. **Geographic alignment** — do they have presence where CC operates (Grand Rapids, LA)?
2. **Program fit** — have they sponsored similar programs (youth tech, community education)?
3. **Fee structure** — typically 5-10% of funds received. Negotiate lower rates for larger budgets.
4. **Service level** — what do they actually provide? (accounting, HR, insurance, grant management, or just a pass-through?)
5. **Autonomy** — how much control does CC retain over hiring, spending, programming?
6. **Grant compatibility** — can CC apply for grants directly under their umbrella, or must the sponsor be the applicant of record?
7. **Transition support** — will they help CC spin out to independent 501(c)(3) when ready?

## Recommended Approach

**Start with Model C** through Community Partners (for LA pilot) or Social Good Fund (national). This gives CC:
- Immediate ability to receive grants and tax-deductible donations
- Autonomy to build organizational capacity from day one
- A clear path to independent 501(c)(3) when annual revenue justifies it (typically $250K+/year)

**Consider separate sponsors for each pilot geography** if a single national sponsor cannot serve both Grand Rapids and LA effectively. Grand Rapids Community Foundation may also offer fiscal sponsorship for the MI pilot specifically.

## Transition to Independent 501(c)(3)

- **When**: Annual revenue consistently exceeds $250K and CC has the operational capacity to handle compliance independently
- **Timeline**: 3-12 months for IRS determination (currently averaging 6-8 months)
- **Cost**: $600 filing fee (Form 1023) plus legal review ($2K-$5K)
- **Trend**: Only 30-40% of fiscally sponsored projects ultimately seek independent status. Many operate under fiscal sponsorship for 2-5 years or indefinitely. There is no shame in staying under a sponsor — it is a legitimate permanent structure.
