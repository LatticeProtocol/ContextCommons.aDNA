# What Is Lattice? — A Community Guide

> **DRAFT** — This file has not been reviewed by community members. Written *for* non-technical readers. `#needs-community-review`

## The Short Version

Lattice is the network that connects your community's AI tools together — and, when you choose, connects your community's tools with other communities' tools. Think of it as the road system between buildings: aDNA organizes the knowledge inside each building, and Lattice connects the buildings to each other.

## What Can Lattice-Based Tools Do for Your Community?

These are real examples drawn from the needs of our pilot communities:

### Help Elders Navigate Complex Systems

Mrs. Johnson at Peter's Church needs to enroll in Medicare Part D. The paperwork is confusing. The deadlines are strict. A Lattice-based agent — built by a youth builder in her own congregation — walks her through the process step by step, in her own language, using information organized by people who know her community.

### Help Families Manage Immigration Paperwork

A family in the LAUSD community needs to organize immigration documents, track deadlines, find translation services, and understand their options. A Lattice agent built by a youth builder at the Venice Beach Book Club connects the right information to the right steps — in Spanish and English — and flags deadlines before they pass.

### Connect People Who Need Help with People Who Can Help

A church coordinator manages dozens of requests: rides to appointments, meal deliveries, tutoring, translation help. A Lattice agent reads the community's context graph and suggests matches — connecting Mrs. Chen (who needs a ride to her doctor) with Mr. Davis (who drives that route on Wednesdays and has volunteered).

### Support Youth Builders in Their Learning

A 16-year-old builder is working through the Builder Track curriculum. Their Lattice agent knows what modules they have completed, what they are working on, what they are struggling with, and what comes next — helping their mentor focus time where it matters most.

## Who Controls These Tools?

**Your community does.** This is the most important thing to understand about Lattice.

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Who decides what the tools can do? | Your community's stewards — pastors, teachers, organizers |
| Who sees the information? | Only the people your community authorizes |
| Can a tech company take your data? | No. Community data belongs to the community. This is a non-negotiable rule. |
| Can a tech company change how the tools work? | No. Technology partners contribute resources (hardware, training), not direction. |
| What if your community wants to stop using the tools? | Your knowledge stays with you. The context graph is yours. |

The governance model is simple: **community stewards make the rules, youth builders build the tools, elders use and shape the tools, and no outsider overrides any of them.**

## Can Communities Work Together?

Yes — this is called **federation**, and it works like a lending library:

- Your community builds a context graph for healthcare navigation that works well in Grand Rapids
- Another community in Detroit is starting a similar program
- Your community can **share** the healthcare navigation graph with Detroit — not give it away, not lose control of it, but let Detroit copy and adapt it for their own context
- Detroit's adaptations stay with Detroit. Your original stays with you. Both communities benefit

Federation means communities can learn from each other without any single community — or any company — controlling the shared knowledge. Each community decides what to share and what to keep private.

### What Sharing Looks Like in Practice

| What Can Be Shared | What Stays Private |
|---|---|
| Curriculum modules and lesson plans | Individual student progress and personal information |
| Context graph templates (e.g., "healthcare navigation structure") | Actual community member data (names, health info, addresses) |
| Agent configurations and workflow designs | Community governance decisions and internal discussions |
| Best practices and lessons learned | Financial details and partnership terms |

## What Does My Community Need to Get Started?

The Context Commons program provides the training, tools, and support. Your community provides:

1. **Willing stewards** — trusted leaders who are curious about what AI tools can do for your community and ready to govern how they are used. They do not need to be technical. They need to be trusted and committed.

2. **Young people ready to build** — ages 14-24, interested in learning to create tools that serve their community. No prior technical experience required. The program teaches everything from the ground up.

3. **Elders and community members with real needs** — people navigating healthcare, immigration, education, or community coordination challenges. Their needs and wisdom are what make the tools useful.

4. **A meeting space** — a church hall, community center, library room, or school classroom where the group can meet regularly.

5. **Basic internet access** — the tools work offline too, but internet connectivity helps. Unreliable internet is fine — the system is designed for it.

That is it. No prior technology infrastructure. No budget for software licenses. No IT department. The program provides the hardware (MacBook Neos for youth builders), the training, the curriculum, and the ongoing technical support.

## Glossary

| Term | Plain-Language Definition |
|------|--------------------------|
| **Lattice** | The network that connects your community's AI tools together and, optionally, with other communities' tools |
| **Federation** | Sharing knowledge and tools between communities without giving up control — like a lending library |
| **Workflow** | A step-by-step process that an AI tool follows to complete a task — like a recipe for getting things done |
| **Node** | A single point in the Lattice network — could be your community's system, a school's system, or a partner's system |
| **Module** | A single building block — one specific capability that an AI tool can use (e.g., "look up Medicare deadlines") |
| **Pilot** | A test run of the program in a real community — Grand Rapids (Pilot A) and Los Angeles (Pilot B) are the first two |
| **Workstation** | The MacBook Neo that a youth builder earns — their personal tool for building and operating AI systems |
| **Capstone** | The final project that a youth builder creates — a real, working AI system that serves a specific community need |
