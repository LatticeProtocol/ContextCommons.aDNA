# What Is aDNA? — A Plain-Language Guide

> **DRAFT** — This file has not been reviewed by community members. It is written *for* non-technical readers, but it needs non-technical readers to confirm it actually makes sense. `#needs-community-review`

## The Short Version

aDNA is a way of organizing your community's knowledge so that AI tools can understand it and help with it — on your terms, under your control.

Think of it like a filing system. But instead of folders full of paper that only humans can read, aDNA creates a structured picture of your community that both people and AI tools can work with.

When Gutenberg invented the printing press, the power was not in any single book — it was that communities could produce and share their own knowledge on their own terms. Churches printed their own Bibles. Guilds published their own manuals. Universities created their own textbooks. aDNA is the same idea for the AI age: your community's knowledge, organized your way, powering tools that serve your people. The printing press did not replace writers. aDNA does not replace the wisdom in your community. It gives that wisdom a form that modern tools can read and act on.

## What Problem Does This Solve?

Most AI tools today work like this: you type a question, the AI guesses an answer based on what it was trained on, and you hope it is right. The AI does not know your community, your people, your rules, or your needs. It is a stranger offering advice.

aDNA changes this. When your community builds a **context graph** — a structured picture of who your people are, what they need, and how things work — an AI tool can read that picture and act on it faithfully. The AI is no longer guessing. It is following your community's map.

## The Three Questions

aDNA organizes everything into three simple questions:

| Question | What Goes Here | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| **WHO?** | People, roles, organizations | "Mrs. Johnson is an elder who needs help with Medicare. Pastor Williams is the steward. Maria is the youth builder assigned to help." |
| **WHAT?** | Knowledge, documents, rules, needs | "Medicare Part D enrollment deadline is December 7. The forms are at medicare.gov. Mrs. Johnson prefers Spanish-language materials." |
| **HOW?** | Processes, steps, workflows | "Step 1: Review current coverage. Step 2: Compare plans on medicare.gov. Step 3: Enroll by deadline. If stuck, escalate to Pastor Williams." |

Everything fits into one of these three categories. If you can answer WHO, WHAT, and HOW, you have described your community's context in a way that AI tools can work with.

Think of it like a church directory — but richer. A church directory tells you who people are. aDNA also tells you what they need and how things get done. It is the directory, the policy manual, and the operations guide, all connected together.

## What Is a Context Graph?

A **context graph** is the structured picture that aDNA creates. It connects the WHO, WHAT, and HOW into a network of relationships:

- Mrs. Johnson (WHO) **needs help with** Medicare enrollment (WHAT)
- Maria (WHO) **follows the process** for healthcare navigation (HOW)
- Pastor Williams (WHO) **oversees** community health programs (HOW)
- Medicare Part D (WHAT) **has a deadline** of December 7 (WHAT)

When an AI tool reads this context graph, it knows who needs help, what the problem is, and how to approach it — because your community told it. The AI is not inventing answers. It is reading your community's organized knowledge.

## What Does This Look Like in Practice?

Here are two scenes — one from each pilot community — showing how building a context graph actually works:

### At Peter's Church, Grand Rapids

**Tuesday evening, church fellowship hall.** Pastor Williams and three youth builders — Maria (17), James (15), and Aisha (16) — sit around a table with a mentor from the Lattice community. They are building a context graph for the church's elder care program.

Pastor Williams starts with WHO: "We have about twenty elders who need regular help. Mrs. Johnson, Mr. Chen, Mrs. Okafor..." He describes each person — not their medical details, but the kind of help they need and who they trust.

Maria types this into the context graph. She asks: "Pastor, when Mrs. Johnson needs a ride to the doctor, who does she call first?" This is HOW — the process, the chain of trust.

"She calls the church office. Then Deacon Harris checks the volunteer schedule." Now the context graph has a workflow: elder needs ride → church office → Deacon Harris → volunteer match.

James adds the WHAT: the volunteer schedule itself, the list of drivers, the regular appointment days.

Aisha notices a gap: "What happens if nobody is available?" Pastor Williams thinks about it: "We call her daughter. If her daughter cannot help, I drive her myself." Now the context graph has an escalation path — a backup plan that the AI tool will know about.

In thirty minutes, the team has mapped one slice of community life. The AI tool that reads this graph will know who needs rides, who can give them, and what to do when the first plan falls through. Not because it guessed — because the community told it.

### At a School in Los Angeles

**Saturday morning, Venice Beach Book Club meeting room.** Two youth builders from LAUSD — Sofia (16) and Daniel (17) — work with a UCLA mentor and a book club coordinator. They are building a context graph for immigration document navigation.

The coordinator explains: "We have families who need to organize papers for DACA renewals, visa applications, work permits. They come to the book club because they trust us, but we are not lawyers."

Sofia maps the WHO: the families who need help, the book club volunteers who speak Spanish and English, the legal aid clinic that takes referrals.

Daniel maps the WHAT: the documents each process requires, the deadlines, the fee waivers available, the nearest USCIS office hours.

The mentor asks: "What happens when a family's situation is too complex for the agent to help with?" The coordinator answers: "We refer them to the legal aid clinic on Lincoln Boulevard. If it is urgent, we call the immigration attorney who volunteers with us on Tuesdays."

Now the context graph has the same structure — WHO, WHAT, HOW — but shaped by a different community with different needs. The same aDNA standard, adapted to a different place.

## What Is an Agent?

In the Context Commons, an **agent** is an AI tool that reads your community's context graph and helps with specific tasks. An agent is not a robot. It is not a person. It is more like a very capable assistant who:

- **Knows what you told it** — only what is in the context graph, nothing more
- **Follows your rules** — the community decides what the agent can and cannot do
- **Works for your people** — the agent serves the community, not a company
- **Can be updated** — when your community's needs change, the context graph changes, and the agent changes with it

An agent might help an elder fill out Medicare paperwork. Another agent might help a family organize immigration documents. Another might help a church coordinate volunteer schedules. Each agent reads a different part of the context graph and does a different job — all under community control.

## How Is This Different from an App?

| | A Typical App | A Community Agent System |
|---|---|---|
| **Who built it?** | A company, for profit | Youth builders in your community, with mentors |
| **Who controls it?** | The company | Your community's stewards |
| **What does it know?** | Whatever the company trained it on | Whatever your community puts in the context graph |
| **What happens when your needs change?** | You wait for the company to update | Your community updates the context graph |
| **What happens when funding runs out?** | The app shuts down | The knowledge stays with your community |
| **Who owns the data?** | Usually the company | Your community — always |

## Common Questions

**"Is this replacing people?"**
No. The AI tool does not replace Mrs. Johnson's daughter, or Deacon Harris, or Pastor Williams. It helps them coordinate. When the volunteer schedule is complicated and the phone tree is long, the agent keeps track so that people can focus on caring for each other instead of juggling spreadsheets.

**"Do I need to be technical to participate?"**
No. Stewards and elders do not write code. They share their knowledge — who needs help, what the community's rules are, how things work. Youth builders (with mentor support) handle the technical side. The Elder & Community Member Track is designed specifically for non-technical participants, with no prerequisites.

**"What if we don't have good internet?"**
The system is designed for unreliable connectivity. Context graphs work offline. When internet is available, the system syncs. When it is not, everything still functions locally. This is a design choice, not an afterthought — we built for the community with spotty church Wi-Fi, not the one with a fiber connection.

**"Who sees our information?"**
Only the people your community authorizes. Your context graph belongs to your community. No tech company can access it, sell it, or use it to train their models. Community stewards set the rules about who sees what. This is non-negotiable.

**"What if we want to stop?"**
You keep everything. The context graph is yours. The knowledge your community organized does not disappear. You can stop using the AI tools and still have a well-organized picture of your community's knowledge — which is valuable on its own.

## Where These Ideas Come From

The Context Commons builds on ideas that communities have practiced for generations:

- **Commons governance** — the principle that communities can manage shared resources collectively, without private ownership or government control. Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize for documenting how communities around the world have governed shared resources successfully for centuries. aDNA applies this to knowledge and AI tools.
- **Learning by doing** — the idea that people learn best by building real things for real purposes, not by sitting through lectures. Paulo Freire called this "praxis" — action and reflection together. The Context Commons curriculum is built on this: youth builders learn by creating tools their communities actually use.
- **Intergenerational knowledge transfer** — every community already does this. Elders teach wisdom; young people teach technology. aDNA gives this natural exchange a structure that AI tools can participate in.

## Glossary

| Term | Plain-Language Definition |
|------|--------------------------|
| **aDNA** | A standard way to organize community knowledge so AI tools can understand and use it |
| **Context graph** | A structured picture of your community — who the people are, what they need, how things work — connected together |
| **Agent** | An AI tool that reads your context graph and helps with specific tasks, following your community's rules |
| **Steward** | A trusted community leader who oversees how AI tools are used in your community |
| **Builder** | A young person (ages 14-24) creating and managing context graphs and agents for their community |
| **Mentor** | An experienced developer who teaches stewards and supports builders |
| **WHO / WHAT / HOW** | The three categories for organizing community knowledge — people, knowledge, and processes |
| **Context** | Organized knowledge about a specific topic — the information an AI tool needs to be helpful |
| **Governance** | The rules and decision-making process your community uses to control its AI tools |
| **Federation** | The ability for communities to share knowledge and tools with each other, without giving up control |
| **Open standard** | A shared set of rules that anyone can use — no single company owns it or controls it |
| **Escalation** | What happens when the first plan does not work — the backup steps, the person to call next |
