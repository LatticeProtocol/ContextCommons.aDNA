# Community Explanation Toolkit

> **DRAFT** — This toolkit is written for stewards, mentors, and anyone introducing the Context Commons to a new audience. It has not been tested with community audiences yet. `#needs-community-review`

## Purpose

This toolkit provides ready-to-use analogies, one-page summaries, and verbal scripts for explaining the Context Commons to non-technical audiences. Every item here has been designed to work in three settings:

1. **Faith-based communities** — church halls, congregation meetings, pastoral conversations (Grand Rapids / Pilot A)
2. **School and book club settings** — classrooms, library meetings, parent-teacher events (Los Angeles / Pilot B)
3. **General community settings** — community centers, civic meetings, neighborhood gatherings

Use what fits. Adapt freely. The goal is not to recite these word-for-word but to have a starting point that respects your audience.

## Five Core Analogies

Each analogy explains one key concept. Use the one that fits your audience and conversation.

### 1. The Printing Press — Community Knowledge Sovereignty

**Concept**: aDNA gives communities control over their own knowledge in the AI age.

**The analogy**: "Before the printing press, only a few powerful institutions controlled written knowledge. Gutenberg changed that — suddenly churches, schools, and guilds could print their own books. AI is at that same moment right now. Most AI tools are controlled by big companies. The Context Commons is about making sure your community has its own printing press — so your knowledge, your rules, and your people's needs are what the AI works from. Not someone else's."

**Best for**: Opening a conversation about why this matters. Works in any setting. Especially resonant in faith communities where the printing press has direct historical meaning (the Gutenberg Bible).

### 2. The Recipe Book — Context Graphs as Organized Knowledge

**Concept**: A context graph is structured community knowledge — like a recipe book, not a pile of ingredients.

**The analogy**: "Imagine your community's knowledge is like ingredients in a kitchen — names, phone numbers, schedules, needs, rules, traditions. Right now, that knowledge lives in people's heads, in filing cabinets, on sticky notes, in group texts. It works, but only if the right person is in the room. A context graph is like turning all of that into a recipe book — organized, findable, and usable by anyone who needs it. The AI reads the recipe book and follows it. It does not invent its own recipes."

**Best for**: Explaining what a context graph is to someone who has never heard the term. The recipe book image is intuitive and non-threatening. Works well with elders and community members who may be skeptical about AI.

### 3. The Church Directory — WHO, WHAT, and HOW

**Concept**: The three-part aDNA structure is like a church directory, but richer.

**The analogy**: "You know your church directory — it tells you who everyone is, maybe their phone number and address. Now imagine a directory that also tells you what people need — Mrs. Johnson needs help with Medicare, the Garcia family needs translation services — and how things get done — who to call first, what the steps are, what to do if the first plan doesn't work. That is what aDNA creates: a living directory that includes the people, the knowledge, and the processes, all connected."

**Best for**: Explaining the WHO/WHAT/HOW structure. Works best in faith-based communities where a church directory is a familiar artifact. In school settings, substitute "school directory" or "community contact list."

### 4. The Lending Library — Federation as Sharing Without Losing

**Concept**: Federation lets communities share templates and learnings without giving up ownership.

**The analogy**: "Think of a lending library. When you lend a book, you still own it. The person who borrows it can read it, learn from it, maybe even make notes for themselves — but they cannot change your copy. Federation works the same way. If Grand Rapids builds a healthcare navigation template that works well, they can share it with a church in Detroit. Detroit copies it, adapts it for their community, and uses it. Grand Rapids still has their original. Neither community controls the other's version."

**Best for**: Explaining how communities can collaborate without losing sovereignty. Addresses the common concern: "If we share our work, will someone else take control of it?"

### 5. The Apprentice Workshop — Youth Builders as Craftspeople

**Concept**: Youth builders learn by building real tools for real communities — like apprentices in a workshop.

**The analogy**: "In the old days, a young person learned a trade by working alongside a master — not by reading a textbook, but by making real things for real customers. A blacksmith's apprentice made real horseshoes. A printer's apprentice set real type. In the Context Commons, youth builders are apprentices. They are not doing homework exercises — they are building real AI tools that their own community actually uses. The mentor guides them. The community steward tells them what to build. And the elder who uses the tool tells them whether it works."

**Best for**: Explaining the program to parents, guardians, or youth themselves. Emphasizes the dignity and seriousness of the work. Counters the perception that this is "just a tech class."

## Steward One-Pagers

### One-Pager A: "What Is the Context Commons?"

*For steward presentations at community meetings, board meetings, or partner conversations.*

**The Context Commons** is a community program that helps your people use AI tools — on your terms, under your control.

**The problem**: Most AI tools do not know your community. They guess. They get things wrong. They are controlled by companies, not by you.

**The solution**: Your community builds a **context graph** — an organized picture of your people (WHO), your knowledge (WHAT), and your processes (HOW). AI tools read this picture and follow it faithfully. The AI works from your community's knowledge, not from a company's guesses.

**How it works**:
- **Youth builders** (ages 14-24) learn to create and manage these tools, earning a MacBook workstation and real-world skills
- **Community stewards** (pastors, teachers, organizers) govern how the tools are used — what the AI can do, who sees information, what gets shared
- **Elders and community members** use the tools and shape them — their needs and wisdom drive what gets built

**What your community provides**: willing leaders, curious young people, community members with real needs, a meeting space, and basic internet access. **The program provides**: hardware, training, curriculum, mentorship, and ongoing technical support.

**What makes this different**: Your community owns the knowledge, controls the tools, and keeps everything if you ever want to stop. No company can take your data, change your rules, or shut down your systems.

### One-Pager B: "What Would My Community Need?"

*Readiness self-assessment for communities considering the program.*

Answer these questions to see whether the Context Commons might be a good fit for your community:

**People**
- [ ] Do you have 2-5 trusted leaders willing to govern how AI tools are used? (These are your stewards.)
- [ ] Do you have 8-16 young people (ages 14-24) interested in learning to build technology? (These are your builders.)
- [ ] Do you have community members (any age) navigating challenges where better information and coordination would help? (These are your users and co-designers.)

**Needs**
- [ ] Does your community have recurring coordination challenges? (Volunteer scheduling, appointment tracking, paperwork assistance, information sharing)
- [ ] Do your members navigate complex systems that are hard to understand? (Healthcare, immigration, education, benefits)
- [ ] Would your community benefit from better organized information that is accessible to helpers and AI tools?

**Space and connectivity**
- [ ] Do you have a regular meeting space where the group can gather? (Church hall, classroom, library room, community center)
- [ ] Do you have some internet access? (Does not need to be reliable — the system is designed for spotty connections)

**Commitment**
- [ ] Can stewards commit to weekly check-ins during the program? (~2 hours/week)
- [ ] Can youth builders commit to the Builder Track? (~6-8 hours/week during active sessions)
- [ ] Is your community willing to try something new and share honest feedback about what works and what does not?

**If you checked most of these boxes**, your community is likely a strong fit. Contact the Context Commons team to discuss next steps.

**If you checked some but not all**, that is normal. Many communities grow into readiness through conversation. Let us talk about what you have and what you would need.

## Verbal Explanation Scripts

### Two-Minute Version

*For quick introductions, hallway conversations, or when someone asks "What is this program?"*

"The Context Commons is a program that helps communities use AI tools — but in a way where the community stays in control.

Right now, most AI tools don't know anything about your community. They guess. We're doing something different: your community organizes its own knowledge — who your people are, what they need, how things get done — and AI tools read that organized knowledge and act on it faithfully.

The program has three roles. Youth builders — young people ages 14 to 24 — learn to create these tools. They earn a MacBook and real skills. Community stewards — trusted leaders like pastors, teachers, organizers — decide how the tools are used. And elders and community members use the tools and tell us whether they actually help.

The community owns everything. No company can take your data or change your rules. If you ever stop using the program, your organized knowledge stays with you.

We're piloting this at Peter's Church in Grand Rapids and with an LAUSD partnership in Los Angeles. We'd love to talk about whether it might work for your community too."

### Five-Minute Version

*For community meetings, board presentations, or partner introductions.*

Start with the two-minute version above, then add:

"Let me give you a concrete example. Imagine an elder in your community — let's call her Mrs. Johnson — needs help enrolling in Medicare Part D. The paperwork is confusing. The deadlines are strict. She's not sure where to start.

Today, she might call the church office, hope someone knows the answer, and get passed around. With the Context Commons, a youth builder in her own congregation has built an AI tool that knows the Medicare process, knows Mrs. Johnson's preferences — she likes materials in Spanish, in large print — and walks her through it step by step. If the tool hits a question it can't answer, it flags it for a human. The community's steward decides what the tool can and cannot do.

Mrs. Johnson didn't need to learn any technology. The youth builder learned real skills by solving a real problem. And the steward made sure it was done responsibly.

Now here's where it gets powerful: that healthcare navigation tool? Another community can borrow the template — like a lending library. They adapt it for their own people, their own hospitals, their own languages. Grand Rapids keeps their version. The other community has their own. Nobody controls anyone else's.

That's the Context Commons: communities owning their knowledge, young people building real tools, elders getting real help, and all of it staying under community control.

What questions do you have?"

## Cultural Adaptation Notes

### Faith-Based Communities (Grand Rapids model)

- Lead with the printing press analogy — it has direct historical resonance (Gutenberg Bible)
- Use "steward" language naturally — stewardship is a familiar concept in church governance
- Frame AI tools as serving pastoral care, not replacing it
- Respect the authority of church leadership in governance discussions
- Example settings: after-service announcements, board meetings, fellowship hall workshops

### School and Book Club Communities (Los Angeles model)

- Lead with the apprentice workshop analogy — emphasizes learning and skill-building
- Use "coordinator" or "program lead" if "steward" feels unfamiliar
- Frame AI tools as supporting educational equity and family navigation
- Acknowledge multi-institutional complexity (school district, book club, university)
- Example settings: parent-teacher nights, book club meetings, classroom introductions, UCLA partnership meetings

### General Community Settings

- Lead with the recipe book analogy — universally accessible, no religious or institutional framing
- Focus on practical benefits: "better organized information, less phone tag, fewer missed deadlines"
- Let the audience's questions guide which deeper analogies to use
- Example settings: neighborhood association meetings, civic forums, partner organization pitches
